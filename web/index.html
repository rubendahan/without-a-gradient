00 The black-box problem

Minimize a function you are not allowed to look inside.

Formally we want x* = argmin f(x) over some search space. Classical optimization assumes structure: a gradient ∇f to follow downhill, convexity to guarantee the bottom is global, an algebraic form to differentiate. Strip all of that away and you are left with the black box: you may submit a candidate x and receive a single number f(x). Nothing else.

This is the rule, not the exception. The "function" might be:

  • a physics simulation scored by an outcome (our traffic case below);
  • a machine-learning model's validation loss as a function of its hyperparameters;
  • a wind-tunnel test, a chemistry assay, a render farm — anything where the output is real but the math is opaque.

Two things make it hard. First, dimensionality: the volume of a search space explodes exponentially, so you can never grid it. Second, multimodality: the landscape is riddled with local minima — valleys that look like the bottom until you climb out and find a deeper one.

The metaheuristic bargain. None of these methods guarantee the global optimum (in general, nothing can without exhausting the space). They are heuristics: clever, biologically- or physically-inspired sampling strategies that spend a fixed budget of evaluations to find a very good solution with high probability. The whole game is the tension between exploration (cover new ground) and exploitation (refine what already looks good). Every algorithm below is a different answer to that one tension.

Throughout, we visualize on 2-D landscapes you can see — but the same code runs in any dimension. The white crosshair marks the true global optimum; watch how each method hunts for it. Drag the sliders while it runs.

01 Particle Swarm Optimization

A flock of candidates, pulled between memory and consensus.

PSO (Kennedy & Eberhart, 1995) was inspired by the way a flock of birds or a school of fish finds food: no individual knows where the food is, yet the group converges on it through purely local rules. Each particle is a candidate solution flying through the search space with a position and a velocity. Two memories steer it:

  • p — its own best position ever (the cognitive pull: "go back to the best I personally found");
  • g — the best position any particle has found (the social pull: "drift toward what the swarm agrees is good").

Every step, each particle updates its velocity then moves:

v ← w·v + c₁·r₁·(p − x) + c₂·r₂·(g − x)
x ← x + v
r₁, r₂ ~ U(0,1) fresh each step  ·  inertia   cognitive   social

Those three terms are the explore/exploit dial. The inertia w is momentum: high w keeps particles roaming (exploration), low w lets them settle (exploitation) — which is why a good PSO anneals w from ~0.9 down to ~0.4 over the run. The cognitive c₁ pulls toward personal memory; the social c₂ toward the group's best. Crank c₂ up and the swarm collapses onto the first decent point it finds — fast, but easily a local trap.

Try this. Set inertia w high (≈1.1) on Rastrigin — the swarm refuses to settle and keeps exploring the ripples. Now drop it to 0.3: it snaps into the nearest basin. Push social c₂ to 3 and cognitive c₁ to 0 — every particle dives at the global best and diversity dies. The sweet spot is a balance, classically c₁ = c₂ ≈ 2 with the constriction factor (here ~1.5 each with clamping).

Why it works — and where it breaks

PSO is almost embarrassingly simple to implement, has few parameters, and is strong on multimodal landscapes because the swarm holds many positions at once. Its failure mode is premature convergence: once g stops improving, the social pull drags everyone in, the difference terms (p − x) and (g − x) shrink to zero, and the swarm freezes — even if the true optimum is elsewhere. The fixes used in the library: a ring topology (particles follow a local neighbour's best, not the global one, so good news spreads slowly and diversity survives), and turbulent re-initialization of the worst particles after a stagnation window. 

# the entire update, vectorized over the swarm (metaheuristics/pso.py)
v = w * v \
  + c1 * r1 * (pbest_x - x) \
  + c2 * r2 * (gbest_x - x)
v = np.clip(v, -v_max, v_max)
x = bounds.clip(x + v)

02 Genetic Algorithms

Darwin as an optimizer: select, recombine, mutate, repeat.

A genetic algorithm (Holland, 1975) treats candidate solutions as individuals in a population and lets natural selection do the searching. Each generation applies three operators that map directly onto biology:

  1. Selection. Pick parents with a bias toward fitness. Tournament selection grabs k random individuals and keeps the best of them; larger k means harsher selection pressure — faster progress, but a quicker collapse of diversity.
  2. Crossover. Recombine two parents into children, mixing their "genes" (coordinates). The intuition — Holland's building-block hypothesis — is that good partial solutions combine into better whole ones. For real numbers, BLX-α samples each child gene from the interval spanned by the parents, slightly extended so children can step a little beyond either parent.
  3. Mutation. Randomly perturb genes. This is the exploration valve: without it the population converges and can only ever recombine what it already has. We use Gaussian mutation with a step that anneals over time.

Elitism copies the few best individuals through untouched, so the best-so-far can never be lost to an unlucky mutation. The three operators split the explore/exploit duty cleanly: crossover exploits by blending good blocks, mutation explores by stepping off the population, and selection supplies the pressure that turns random drift into a directed climb.

Watch the population breathe. Early on it is spread across the whole landscape (exploration); generation by generation, selection pulls it toward the good basins and it contracts (exploitation). Turn mutation σ to near zero and the population collapses fast but can get stuck; turn it high and it never commits, scattering forever. Raise tournament k to feel selection pressure: high k converges hard, low k keeps weak individuals around as genetic raw material.

GA vs. PSO

They are cousins — both population-based — but differ in how information moves. In PSO, particles keep their identity and are attracted toward good points; in a GA, individuals are replaced by recombinations of good ones. GAs shine when the problem has combinatorial structure or deceptive ruggedness where building blocks matter; PSO often converges faster on smooth-ish continuous problems. On a flat multimodal grid like Rastrigin, a GA's crossover-driven diversity frequently finds the global basin where a greedy swarm settles for second best.

03 Differential Evolution

Mutate by the difference between two random members. Self-scaling, almost parameter-free.

Differential Evolution (Storn & Price, 1997) is the connoisseur's default for continuous black-box problems. Its mutation operator is one elegant idea: to perturb a member, add the scaled difference of two other random members. For the classic rand/1/bin scheme, target xᵢ gets a donor

v = xa + F · (xb − xc)
a, b, c distinct random members · F ≈ 0.5–0.9

then binomial crossover mixes donor and target coordinate-by-coordinate (each from the donor with probability CR, at least one guaranteed), and the trial replaces the target only if it scores at least as well — greedy selection.

The beautiful part is self-scaling. Early on the population is spread out, so a difference vector x_b − x_c is large and DE takes big exploratory leaps. As the population converges around the optimum, those differences shrink automatically, and the steps become fine local refinements — no step-size schedule required. The search adapts its own scale to the landscape for free.

Feel the self-scaling. Watch the candidate cloud: it starts coarse and automatically tightens as members agree. F sets step aggressiveness (too low → premature convergence; too high → never settles); CR controls how much of the donor enters each trial — high CR suits separable functions, low CR suits ones with tangled coordinates. On Rosenbrock's curved valley DE is excellent, second only to CMA-ES below.

04 Simulated Annealing

One walker, cooling from chaos into order — and willing to climb uphill to escape a trap.

Simulated Annealing (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983) borrows from metallurgy: heat a metal and its atoms jiggle freely; cool it slowly and they settle into a low-energy crystal. SA is a single point taking random steps, with one crucial twist — it sometimes accepts a worse move. A proposal that improves is always taken; a proposal that worsens by Δ is accepted with probability

P(accept) = exp(−Δ / T)
T = temperature, lowered on a cooling schedule

At high temperature almost any move is accepted — the walker roams freely and hops out of local minima. As T cools toward zero, uphill moves become rare and finally forbidden, so the walker settles into whatever basin it is in. The cooling schedule is everything: cool too fast and you quench into a bad local minimum; cool too slow and you waste your whole budget. Geometric cooling T ← α·T with α near 1 is the standard compromise.

The accept-worse magic. The yellow trail is the walker; red segments are rejected proposals. Early (hot) you'll see it accept moves that go uphill — that's how it escapes the cosine ripples of Rastrigin. With a fast cooling α = 0.90 it freezes early, often in a nearby local minimum; with α = 0.999 it explores far longer before committing. SA is the cheapest way to get the "escape local minima" behaviour — one point, one evaluation per step, no population to maintain.

05 CMA-ES

Don't move points — move a distribution, and learn the shape of the valley.

The Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) is the heavyweight of continuous black-box optimization, and it works at a different level of abstraction than everything above. Instead of pushing individual candidates around, it maintains a multivariate Gaussian distribution N(m, σ²C) over the search space and updates that distribution each generation:

  1. Sample λ candidates from the current Gaussian.
  2. Rank them and keep the best μ.
  3. Recombine — the new mean m is their weighted average.
  4. Adapt the covariance C and step size σ so the Gaussian stretches along the directions that have been producing improvements, and shrinks along the rest.

That covariance adaptation is the magic. On an ill-conditioned, curved valley like Rosenbrock — where the productive direction is a thin diagonal ridge — CMA-ES learns the ridge's orientation and scale and reshapes its sampling ellipse to align with it. It effectively recovers second-order (Newton-like) information about the landscape without ever computing a gradient. Two evolution paths (the cumulative step directions) decouple the control of the overall step size from the shape, which is what makes it so stable.

The trade-off. The covariance update is O(d²) per step (an eigendecomposition of a d×d matrix), so CMA-ES is the tool for low-to-medium dimension (roughly d ≤ 100) where sample efficiency matters more than per-step cost. In the benchmark suite it crushes smooth/ill-conditioned problems — Rosenbrock to 10⁻²³, far past anything else — but a regular multimodal grid like Rastrigin can still trap its single adapting cloud, where a diverse population wins. No demo here (a drifting, reshaping ellipse in 2-D undersells it), but the full implementation is in metaheuristics/cma_es.py, ~90 readable lines.

06 Bayesian Optimization

When every evaluation is precious, spend compute to think instead of to sample.

Everything so far burns through thousands of evaluations. But what if a single f(x) costs an hour of simulation, a day of GPU training, or a real physical experiment? Then you can afford only a few dozen, and you must choose each one wisely. Bayesian optimization is the answer, and it works by building a cheap model of the expensive function:

  1. Surrogate. Fit a Gaussian Process to the points seen so far. A GP returns, for any input, both a predicted mean μ(x) and an honest uncertainty σ(x) — wide where you have no data, tight where you've measured.
  2. Acquisition. Use that uncertainty to decide where to look next. Expected Improvement scores each candidate by how much it might beat the current best, integrating over the GP's uncertainty:
    EI(x) = (fbest − μ(x))·Φ(z) + σ(x)·φ(z),   z = (fbest − μ(x)) / σ(x)
    The first term rewards a low predicted mean (exploitation); the second rewards high uncertainty (exploration). EI automatically balances them.
  3. Evaluate the true f at the EI maximizer, add the point, refit, repeat.
Watch it reason. The dashed grey curve is the hidden function (the algorithm cannot see it). Blue is the GP's mean belief; the blue band is its ±2σ uncertainty. Green at the bottom is Expected Improvement, and the white line is where it will sample next. Hit "sample next" and watch the band collapse around the new point and EI redistribute. Notice it doesn't just drill into the current best — it keeps probing wide, uncertain regions, because EI values the chance of a surprise. length scale sets how smooth it assumes f is; ξ tilts the explore/exploit balance.

The pay-off is dramatic sample efficiency: tens of evaluations where a swarm would use thousands. The cost is the O(n³) GP fit, which is exactly why BO is reserved for the regime where f is so expensive that n stays small. It is the standard tool behind automated hyperparameter tuning.

07 So which one do I use?

There is no universal winner — and that's a theorem, not an excuse.

The No Free Lunch theorem (Wolpert & Macready, 1997) proves that averaged over all possible objective functions, every optimizer performs identically. A method only wins by matching the structure of your particular problem. So the real question is never "what's the best algorithm" but "what do I know about my landscape and my budget?"

A practical decision tree: Is each evaluation expensive? → Bayesian optimization. Otherwise, is the landscape smooth and ill-conditioned, in modest dimension? → CMA-ES. Otherwise, for a rugged multimodal continuous problem, reach for DE, PSO, or a GA — and always run random-restart hill climbing as the baseline they must beat. If a fancy method can't beat random restarts, it isn't earning its complexity.

The meta-lesson. In practice the biggest wins rarely come from the algorithm choice. They come from (1) a good representation of the problem, (2) warm starts from a sensible heuristic instead of random initialization, and (3) a fast surrogate so you can afford more evaluations. The optimizer is the last 10%.

08 Where this came from

An honest postmortem of the hackathon that produced this toolbox.

This library was not built in the abstract. It came out of the Delta 2026 hackathon, whose Task D asked us to time the 341 traffic lights of a city road network to minimize the total delay of 5,215 vehicles over a four-hour mesoscopic simulation. Each light has phases, green durations, and an offset — so the decision vector lived in a few hundred dimensions, and the only way to score a candidate was to run the whole simulation. A textbook expensive, high-dimensional black box.

So we threw the entire zoo at it: a multi-swarm PSO with exploratory, balanced, and exploitative sub-swarms; genetic algorithms over offsets and phase orderings; differential evolution and simulated annealing on a fast analytical proxy; Bayesian optimization with random embeddings to fight the dimensionality; even a Navier–Stokes fluid analogy for green-time allocation. The winning recipe was a multi-swarm PSO seeded from a green-wave heuristic, polished by a simulation-feedback loop.

The twist. Once we reverse-engineered the official simulator, the problem turned out to be nearly trivial: with the given demand the network was so under-saturated that almost any sane configuration — essentially "all-green, never stop anyone" — was within a fraction of a percent of optimal. The elaborate optimizer tournament was, for that instance, overkill.

Which is exactly why this site exists. The contest answer was throwaway, but the toolbox and the understanding were not. We pulled the algorithms out of the one-off hackathon code, rewrote them cleanly against standard benchmark functions where their real character shows, tested them, and wrote up the theory you just read. The lesson the hackathon taught — understand your problem before you optimize it — is the same one in the box above. The optimizer is the last 10%.
